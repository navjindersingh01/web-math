Mathematical Series

A series is the sum of the terms of a sequence. Understand finite sums, convergence, and divergence through guided calculators with full step-by-step working.

Series vs. Sequence

A sequence lists numbers in order: 1, 2, 3, 4… A series adds them: 1+2+3+4+… = Sₙ. Series can be finite (fixed upper bound) or infinite. An infinite series either converges to a finite limit or diverges to ±∞.

Determining convergence is one of the central questions of calculus and real analysis — with profound implications in physics, engineering, and computer science.

Series Calculators

Arithmetic Series (AP)

Sum of n terms with first term a and common difference d.
Sₙ = n/2 · (2a + (n−1)d)
+
Young Gauss summed 1 to 100 = 5050 in seconds by pairing: (1+100)+(2+99)+… = 50 pairs of 101. The formula encodes this: Sₙ = n × (average of first and last term).

Geometric Series (GP)

Sum of n terms of a geometric sequence (r ≠ 1).
Sₙ = a(1 − rⁿ) / (1 − r)
+
Compound interest over n periods is a GP series. Also models binary storage: 1, 2, 4, 8… bytes (a=1, r=2). When r = 1, use Sₙ = n × a.

Infinite Geometric Series

Converges only when |r| < 1.
S∞ = a / (1 − r)
+
Zeno's Paradox: to cross a room you must first cross half, then half of the rest… infinitely. Yet you arrive — because ½+¼+⅛+… = 1. This converges to a/(1−r) only when |r| < 1.

Harmonic Series

1 + ½ + ⅓ + ¼ + … — grows without bound despite shrinking terms.
Hₙ = Σ 1/k, k = 1 to n
+
Proved divergent by Nicole Oresme in the 14th century. The partial sum Hₙ ≈ ln(n) + γ, where γ ≈ 0.5772 is the Euler–Mascheroni constant. A counterintuitive and beautiful result of analysis.
